What is this page? Each element detail page shows a set of scientific measurements and classifications. This glossary explains what each one means, how it is measured, and why it matters — in plain language.
Table Structure
Atomic Number
Symbol: Z

The atomic number is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It is the single most important property of an element because it uniquely defines what element you are dealing with. Every atom of oxygen has exactly 8 protons — if it had 7 it would be nitrogen, and if it had 9 it would be fluorine.

In a neutral atom (no electric charge), the atomic number also equals the number of electrons orbiting the nucleus. Electrons are what determine how an atom bonds and reacts with others.

The periodic table is arranged in order of increasing atomic number, from hydrogen (Z=1) to oganesson (Z=118).

Example Carbon has atomic number 6 → 6 protons in every carbon nucleus, 6 electrons in a neutral carbon atom.
Period
Horizontal row 1 – 7

A period is a horizontal row of the periodic table. All elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells (also called energy levels). Period 1 has 1 shell, Period 2 has 2 shells, and so on up to Period 7.

Moving left to right across a period, each element gains one more proton and one more electron. This causes a pattern: elements on the left tend to be reactive metals; those on the right tend to be reactive nonmetals; and the far right is always a chemically inert noble gas.

Periods 1–3 are "short periods" (2 or 8 elements). Periods 4–5 are "long periods" (18 elements, including the d-block transition metals). Periods 6–7 are "very long periods" (32 elements each, including the f-block lanthanides and actinides).

Example Period 2: Li, Be, B, C, N, O, F, Ne — all have 2 electron shells. Atomic radius shrinks and electronegativity rises from left to right.
Group
Vertical column 1 – 18

A group (also called a family) is a vertical column of the periodic table. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons (outer-shell electrons), which means they tend to have very similar chemical properties and react in similar ways.

Group 1 elements (alkali metals) all have 1 valence electron and are all highly reactive metals. Group 18 elements (noble gases) all have a full outer shell and are almost completely inert. This is why Mendeleev could predict undiscovered elements — he knew the pattern of the columns.

Groups 3 through 12 are the transition metals (d-block). The f-block elements (lanthanides and actinides) are extracted below the main table and technically belong at Group 3.

Example Group 17 (Halogens): F, Cl, Br, I, At — all have 7 valence electrons, all form -1 ions, all react vigorously with alkali metals to form salts.

Atomic Properties
Atomic Mass
Unit: u (atomic mass unit)  |  also called atomic weight

Atomic mass is the average mass of one atom of an element, measured in atomic mass units (u, also written amu or Da for Dalton). One atomic mass unit is defined as exactly 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

The mass of an atom comes almost entirely from its nucleus. Protons and neutrons each weigh approximately 1 u. Electrons are about 1,836 times lighter and contribute almost nothing to the total mass.

The reason atomic mass is rarely a whole number is that most elements exist as a mixture of isotopes — atoms with different numbers of neutrons. The atomic mass shown on the periodic table is the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes, weighted by how abundant each is in nature.

Example — Chlorine (Cl) Cl-35 (75.77% abundant) + Cl-37 (24.23% abundant) Weighted average = (35 × 0.7577) + (37 × 0.2423) = 35.45 u That is why the periodic table shows 35.453 — not 35 or 37.
Isotopes
Same element, different neutron count

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons in their nuclei. Because they have the same number of protons and electrons, isotopes of the same element behave almost identically in chemical reactions — but they have different masses and different nuclear stability.

Isotopes are written as: Element-MassNumber. For example, Carbon-12 (C-12) has 6 protons and 6 neutrons. Carbon-14 (C-14) has 6 protons and 8 neutrons — it is radioactive and is the basis of radiocarbon dating.

Some elements have only one stable isotope (e.g. fluorine has only F-19). Others have many — tin has 10 stable isotopes, the most of any element.

Hydrogen Isotopes H-1 (Protium) — 1 proton, 0 neutrons — 99.98% of all hydrogen H-2 (Deuterium) — 1 proton, 1 neutron — heavy water uses this H-3 (Tritium) — 1 proton, 2 neutrons — radioactive, half-life 12.3 years
Radioactivity
Unstable nuclei that decay over time

A nucleus is radioactive when it is unstable — meaning it will spontaneously emit particles or energy (radiation) to reach a more stable state. This process is called radioactive decay. The time it takes for half of a sample to decay is called its half-life, which can range from a fraction of a second to billions of years.

There are three main types of radiation emitted during decay: alpha particles (2 protons + 2 neutrons, stopped by paper), beta particles (fast electrons, stopped by aluminum), and gamma rays (high-energy photons, require thick lead or concrete to stop).

All elements heavier than bismuth (atomic number 83) have no stable isotopes — all their forms are radioactive. Elements with very short half-lives, like francium (22 minutes) or oganesson (0.89 milliseconds), are essentially undetectable in nature.

Half-life examples U-238 (Uranium) — 4.47 billion years (older than Earth) C-14 (Carbon) — 5,730 years (used in dating fossils) Fr-223 (Francium)— 22.3 minutes Og-294 (Oganesson)— 0.89 milliseconds
Synthetic Elements
Made only in laboratories — shown with RED symbols

Synthetic elements are those that do not occur in nature in any significant quantity and must be produced artificially, typically by bombarding heavy nuclei with smaller particles in a particle accelerator or nuclear reactor.

On this table, synthetic element symbols are shown in red. They include technetium (Tc, #43) — the first element to be produced synthetically, in 1937 — promethium (Pm, #61), and all elements from neptunium (#93) onward.

The heaviest synthetic elements, sometimes called "superheavy" elements (roughly #104 and above), exist for only milliseconds to seconds after being created. Only a handful of atoms of each have ever been detected. Their chemical properties are largely unknown.

Notable synthetic elements Tc-99m (Technetium) — most widely used medical imaging isotope Pu-239 (Plutonium) — nuclear reactor fuel and weapons Am-241 (Americium) — inside household smoke detectors Og-118 (Oganesson) — only 5 atoms ever detected
Electronic Structure
Electron Configuration
How electrons are arranged in an atom

The electron configuration describes where all the electrons of an atom are located, organized by shell (energy level) and subshell (orbital type). Electrons fill the lowest available energy levels first.

The notation uses numbers for shells and letters for subshell types, with superscripts for the count of electrons in each subshell: shell subshell ⊃electrons.

2p⁴ Shell 2 • p-subshell • 4 electrons
[He] 2s² 2p⁴ Full config for Oxygen (#8)

The [He] shorthand means "same as helium's configuration plus the following." This makes long configurations readable. The last subshell listed tells you the most about the element's chemistry — those are the valence electrons.

Reading examples H (#1) : 1s¹ — 1 electron in the 1s subshell O (#8) : [He] 2s² 2p⁴ — helium core + 6 more electrons Au (#79) : [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s¹ — gold's complex relativistic config
Valence Electrons
The outermost electrons — the ones that bond

Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell of an atom. They are the only electrons that participate in chemical bonding — forming the connections between atoms in molecules and compounds. The number of valence electrons is the single most important factor in determining an element's chemical behavior.

For main-group elements (Groups 1, 2, and 13–18), the group number directly tells you the number of valence electrons: Group 1 = 1 valence electron, Group 2 = 2, Group 13 = 3, and so on up to Group 18 = 8 (full shell, very stable — noble gases).

Atoms with almost-full shells (like halogens with 7 valence electrons) strongly want to gain 1 electron. Atoms with almost-empty shells (like alkali metals with 1) strongly want to lose 1. This tendency drives chemical reactions.

Example Sodium (Na): [Ne] 3s¹ — 1 valence electron, wants to lose it → Na⁺ ion Chlorine (Cl): [Ne] 3s² 3p⁵ — 7 valence electrons, wants 1 more → Cl⁻ ion Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl (table salt) — stable compound
Orbital Types: s, p, d, f
The shapes of electron probability clouds

Electrons do not orbit the nucleus like planets. Instead, each electron occupies an orbital — a region of space where there is a high probability of finding that electron. Orbitals come in four shapes, labeled s, p, d, and f.

s orbitals — spherical. One per subshell, holds up to 2 electrons. Found in every shell (1s, 2s, 3s…)

p orbitals — dumbbell-shaped (two lobes). Three per subshell (along x, y, z axes), holds up to 6 electrons. Start at shell 2 (2p, 3p…)

d orbitals — four-leaf clover shapes. Five per subshell, holds up to 10 electrons. Start at shell 3 (3d, 4d…). These are what make transition metals distinctive.

f orbitals — complex six-lobe shapes. Seven per subshell, holds up to 14 electrons. Start at shell 4 (4f, 5f). These fill in the lanthanides and actinides.

Maximum electrons per subshell s → 2 electrons (1 orbital × 2) p → 6 electrons (3 orbitals × 2) d → 10 electrons (5 orbitals × 2) f → 14 electrons (7 orbitals × 2)
Physical Properties
Standard State
Physical form at 25°C and 1 atmosphere of pressure

The standard state of an element is its physical form under standard conditions: a temperature of 25°C (77°F) and a pressure of exactly 1 atmosphere (sea level air pressure). It answers the simple question: is this element a solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature?

Solid — atoms/molecules are tightly packed in a fixed structure. Most elements are solids at room temperature, including all metals.

Liquid — atoms/molecules flow freely but stay in contact. Only two elements are liquid at standard conditions: mercury (Hg) and bromine (Br).

Gas — atoms/molecules move freely and spread to fill any container. Eleven elements are gases at room temperature: H, N, O, F, Cl (as diatomic molecules), and the six noble gases He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn.

The two room-temperature liquids Mercury (Hg, #80) — the only liquid metal; melting point -38.8°C Bromine (Br, #35) — the only liquid nonmetal; melting point -7.2°C
Melting Point
Temperature at which solid becomes liquid

The melting point is the temperature at which a solid element transforms into a liquid (at standard pressure). At exactly the melting point, solid and liquid can coexist in equilibrium. Below it, the element is solid; above it, liquid.

Melting point reflects how strongly atoms are held together in the solid structure. Tungsten (W) has the highest melting point of all elements at 3,422°C — its atoms are held together so tightly it is used in light bulb filaments and rocket nozzles. Helium has the lowest, never becoming solid under normal pressure at any temperature.

Temperatures on element pages are given in degrees Celsius (°C). To convert: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32. To convert to Kelvin (used in science): K = °C + 273.15.

Extremes Highest melting point: Tungsten (W) — 3,422°C (6,192°F) Lowest melting point: Mercury (Hg) — -38.8°C (-37.9°F) Helium (He) has no melting point at normal pressure — stays liquid to absolute zero
Boiling Point
Temperature at which liquid becomes gas

The boiling point is the temperature at which a liquid element transforms into a gas (at standard pressure, 1 atm). At the boiling point, the vapor pressure of the liquid equals atmospheric pressure, allowing bubbles to form throughout the liquid.

The gap between melting point and boiling point tells you how wide the liquid range is. Mercury has a liquid range of only about 357°C (from -39°C to 357°C). Tungsten's liquid range spans over 2,000°C. Elements that are gases at room temperature typically have both melting points and boiling points well below 0°C.

Noble gas boiling points (all gases, all very cold) Helium (He) — -268.9°C (just 4 degrees above absolute zero) Neon (Ne) — -246.1°C Argon (Ar) — -185.9°C Xenon (Xe) — -108.1°C (used in anesthesia and photography)
Density
Mass per unit volume — g/cm³ or g/L for gases

Density is how much mass is packed into a given volume. It is calculated as: Density = Mass ÷ Volume. For solid and liquid elements, density is measured in grams per cubic centimeter (g/cm³). For gases at standard conditions, it is usually given in grams per liter (g/L).

For solids and liquids, density is measured in grams per cubic centimeter (g/cm³). For gases, it is measured in grams per liter (g/L) since gases are far more spread out. Water has a density of 1.0 g/cm³ — this is a useful reference point. Any element with a density below 1.0 g/cm³ floats on water (lithium, sodium, and potassium all do, though they react violently). Elements with higher density sink. Osmium (Os) is the densest element at 22.59 g/cm³ — a cube of osmium the size of a golf ball would weigh about 2 kg (4.4 lbs).

Gas densities appear very small because gas molecules are spread far apart. Hydrogen gas (H₂) has a density of only 0.0000899 g/cm³ — about 800 times less dense than water.

Density extremes Densest element: Osmium (Os) — 22.59 g/cm³ Least dense solid: Lithium (Li) — 0.534 g/cm³ (floats on water) Least dense gas: Hydrogen (H₂) — 0.0000899 g/cm³
Atomic Radius
Unit: pm (picometers) — 1 pm = one trillionth of a meter

Atomic radius is a measure of the size of an atom — specifically, half the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms that are just touching. Because atoms do not have a hard edge (the electron cloud fades out gradually), radius is always a practical measurement that depends on context.

There are two common versions used on this site. The covalent radius is used for atoms that form chemical bonds: it is half the distance between two identical bonded atoms (e.g., half the C—C bond length in diamond gives carbon's covalent radius of 73 pm). The van der Waals radius is used for atoms that do not normally form bonds, primarily noble gases: it is half the distance between two non-bonded atoms gently touching.

Atomic radius follows a clear and predictable pattern:

• Across a period left to right: radius shrinks. Each element gains one more proton, pulling all electrons tighter, even though they stay in the same shell. Fluorine is much smaller than lithium despite both being in Period 2.

• Down a group top to bottom: radius grows. Each new period adds a whole new electron shell, physically expanding the atom. Cesium is far larger than lithium despite both being alkali metals.

Period 2 covalent radii — shrinks left to right Li: 128 pm → Be: 96 pm → B: 84 pm → C: 73 pm → N: 71 pm → O: 66 pm → F: 57 pm
Group 1 alkali metals — grows top to bottom Li: 128 pm → Na: 166 pm → K: 203 pm → Rb: 220 pm → Cs: 244 pm
Chemical Properties
Electronegativity — Pauling Scale
Range: ~0.7 (Fr) to 3.98 (F) — dimensionless

Electronegativity measures an atom's tendency to attract electrons toward itself when it is part of a chemical bond. The higher the value, the more strongly an atom pulls shared electrons away from its bonding partner.

The most widely used scale was devised by Linus Pauling in 1932. It is dimensionless (no units) and ranges from roughly 0.7 for francium (least electronegative) to 3.98 for fluorine (most electronegative element of all). Pauling derived the values from experimental bond energies: he assigned fluorine a reference value of ~4.0 and calculated all others relative to it.

Electronegativity determines the type of chemical bond that forms between two atoms based on the difference in their values:

• Difference > 1.7 → ionic bond: the more electronegative atom essentially steals the electron(s). Example: NaCl (Na=0.93, Cl=3.16, difference=2.23) — sodium gives its electron to chlorine.

• Difference 0.4–1.7 → polar covalent bond: electrons are shared unequally, creating a partial positive and negative end. Example: water (H—O difference ≈1.24).

• Difference < 0.4 → nonpolar covalent bond: electrons are shared equally. Example: H—H (difference = 0), O=O (difference = 0).

The trend mirrors atomic radius but in reverse: electronegativity increases left to right across a period (more protons pull electrons harder) and decreases going down a group (outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded by inner shells).

Pauling values — Period 2 (rises left to right) Li: 0.98 → Be: 1.57 → B: 2.04 → C: 2.55 → N: 3.04 → O: 3.44 → F: 3.98
Why noble gases have no Pauling value Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar ...) do not normally form bonds, so there is no bond-energy data from which to calculate an electronegativity. The Pauling scale simply does not apply to them.
